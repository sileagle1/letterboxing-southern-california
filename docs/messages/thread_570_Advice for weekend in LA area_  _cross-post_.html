From: Jim Carleton (chaosmanor47@gmail.com) | Date: 2007-02-16 16:51:29 UTC
--- In letterboxing-socal@yahoogroups.com, "Debbie Kotlarek" wrote:

> Right now we haven't booked a hotel for next weekend, waiting to see

> flexible, but didn't want to spend a lot of hours in the car (which

Indeed! One assumes that the freeways will be open on a Saturday at
your peril :-o Sharon was born in SoCal, and I've lived here for a
half-century, and there are still surprises aplenty when dealing with
L.A. traffic :-( Always allow more time than you think you need, then
add a half-hour.

> We weren't sure if Mt. Baldy would have snow this time of year, and
> if it did, whether the boxes would be accessible.

If conditions don't change dramatically over the next few weeks (i.e.
if we don't much more rain than we've had so far this season), Baldy
and most of the other mountains should be snow-free, or maybe lightly
dusted on the north-facing slopes. Right now, the vast majority of
snow in the San Bernardinos and San Gabriels is man-made on the skiing
slopes, and there is almost none anywhere in the Santa Ynez and Sierra
Pelona. The Santa Monicas and Santa Anas almost never get any, no
matter *how* cold it gets. Thus, I would suggest keeping an eye on
weather.com (use ZIP 90001 as your center; that's in the middle of LA)
and if we don't get a major rainstorm through, you should be able to
hike anywhere you want. Bring some warm stuff for the high altitudes,
but make it easily sheddable. As for particular boxes to go after,
we're not going to be much help, as we live in Ventura County, and
havne't done any boxing in the San Gabriels, etc., but we lived in
Riverside for two decades, with Baldy and other peaks in view when the
smog wasn't real bad, and have done a lot of "just plain hiking" in
them. It's worth it even if you don't find any nboxes at all. With
any luck, you might start to see some wildflowers in the lower
altitudes. I would imagine that any boxes you go for will be worth
the hike, if only because the scenery will likely be different from
what you are used to. And you won't need snowshoes to get to them! ;-)

One suggestion that might help you a lot is to pick up a copy of a
trail book for wherever you happen to decide to go. Any
reasonably-large bookstore in the L.A. area will have many of them.
"101 Hikes in Southern California", by Jerry Schad, from Wilderness
Press, is a good overview of the whole of SoCal, from the Mexican
border up to our area. There are many others that are more detailed,
or that focus on particular areas. Naturally, we have several for the
Santa Monicas, where we do most of our hiking, now. A good trail
guide could also save you a lot of grief.

Before you do any hiking in the mountains, go to the nearest U.S.
Forest Service office and enquire about trail conditions and any
passes needed. Some areas require an Adventure Pass to park.
Day-passes cost five bucks, which is cheap, to my mind, although a lot
of people have expressed outrage (with some validity) about this fee.
To find a Ranger Station, look in the local phone book, or go on-line
at www.fs.fed.us; use the "State" drop-box to get to California, and
then click on Angeles, Cleveland, San Bernardino or Los Padres,
depending upon which you decide to explore. There will be a list of
District Offices for each Forest. This website is one which I
recommend *very strongly* for anyone hiking in any USForest, as it
gets updated pretty quickly for our forests. Imagine! a government
agency that is reasonably efficient :-) Besides the pass and trail
conditions information, you'll also be able to get detailed maps of
the trails and roads.

By the way, bring a compass along, even if you don't plan to go after
any boxes that need one. And if you have a GPSr, the mapping function
on it could save you if you get lost. Always leave a note at your car
as to where you plan to go, when you left and how long you plan to be
gone. You might not think that mountains so close to a huge city like
L.A. could be that dangerous, but bears, rattlesnakes and mountain
lions *do* live in them, so be alert. Make a little noise while
hiking, so that you don't surprise them. Hope I haven't scared you
off, but "Forewarned is fore-armed, which is half an octopus", or
something like that ;-) Hope you enjoy your trip!

Jim

Hello Wisconsin Hiker -

If you are going to be hiking in the Pasadena/Altadena area, you will be right in my neck
of the woods! (We live just a few blocks from the Eaton Canyon Nature Center.) If you are
going to go after the Eaton Canyon Hummingbird and Henninger Flats, you should also
pick up California Bear's/Buzzard's Eaton Canyon box while you are at it.
(http://www.atlasquest.com/lboxes/showboxinfo.html?gBoxId=2754)
Be sure to check weather conditions or with the volunteers in the Nature Center if you are
going after the Hummingbird -- if there has been enough rain or snow up in the
mountains, the creek in the canyon will swell up, making it impassable (or just too wet to
deal with on a cool winter/spring day!).

I also have a box, Cold Feet, planted in the Verdugo Mountains, just to the E of Burbank.
(http://www.atlasquest.com/lboxes/showboxinfo.html?gBoxId=28799)
If you can't read the clue, let me know and I'll send a copy to you. I was super nervous
about planting my first box, and put a F-1 restriction on it, for no great reason.
Anyway, there is whole network of trails in the Verdugo Mountains that make for some
good hiking. But mine is the only box I am aware of in the area, so it would make more
sense to hit the canyons in Altadena/Pasadena if you want a better letterboxing return on
your hike!

If you go after the Echo Mountain series, please do report back on the experience! I have
had my eyes on that hike for a while, but we have a 5-year-old that hikes with us, and he
is good for about 3 miles (hills or no hills), but then he poops out. That series seems like
it would just be too much for him.

Enjoy your trip!
- Sitzmak
(who lived in Madison, WI for six years, and *loved* it!)

Wisconsin Hiker,
I'm pretty sure you've stamped into some of my SoCal boxes before, so
welcome back! I know the fate of a few of the boxes you list below,
so here is my intel:

Henninger Flats: This was one of my first letterbox plants, and I
checked on it periodically for 2 years without a single finder. I
recently found it missing, possibly removed by the Forest Service.
(It was a crappy stamp anyway--one of my first carvings.)

Las Flores Canyon: The first of this series, the Cobb Estate, is
missing. I don't know the fate of the second LB in the series, but
the hike described in the clues passes over a very eroded section of
trail that scared the heck out of me. I did it once (because I can be
incredibly stubborn) and found the letterbox, but wouldn't ever
consider going back because the trail was too scarey. (But it was a
beautiful canyon, Leslie.)

Cooper Canyon: This is a strange story. According to the logbook, I
was the first letterboxer to find this box more then a year after it
was planted. But the day after it was planted, a Boy Scout found the
box, thought it was a Geocache, took the stamp, and left a small
flashlight in exchange, which I found.

Eaton Canyon Hummingbird: This was the 2nd letterbox I ever found,
but the LB didn't last long. I adopted it, and have replaced it
twice. This is a very busy area, so I don't know if my most recent
replacement is still there. (Lovely waterfall though; did you know it
was visited and written about in the 19th Century by John Muir?!)

Passport to the Falls: I've found this LB to be intact on several
trips to the area. I think it's very possible that it is still there.
The other LB planted in this series entitled "A Bridge Too
Fingerprinted" is missing, though.

Echo Mountain Series is gone. Pity. Leslie was so right about this
location. This whole mountain is so beautiful and so historical it
needs a slew of letterboxes. This is a future project for me.

Millard Falls: I have every reason to believe this LB is intact.

The Elyria Canyon letterboxes were not artfully hidden, and were
placed in very high traffic areas, so I expect they may not still be
in place, but I have no confirmation of this.

I would like to think that the two Mount Baldy stamps are still in
place, but they were planted years ago. It's a sad commentary on my
letterboxing skills that I have not already confirmed their status,
but I haven't. I'd love to hear if you find them.

-Buzzard
