THE HALLOWEEN SERIES

Placed by: Buzzard
Placed on: 10/19/03
State: California
County: Los Angeles
Nearest City: Pasadena
Number of boxes: 6

Difficulty: Easy. Dogs on a leash are okay, as are children in
strollers. Wheelchairs can get to some of these letterboxes, please
email me for details.

We Pasadenans love holidays. We put holiday-themed lights on our
houses, fly banners outside our houses, and decorate our lawns with
lighted figures for St. Patrick's Day, July 4th, Easter, Presidents'
Day, and, most of all to celebrate our favorite holidays: Halloween
and Christmas. As a true Pasadenan, I love all holidays, but my all-
time personal favorite is Halloween! By October 7th, the French Hen
and I have decorated our house with Halloween lights, vintage die-
cuts, and special effects (black lights, fog machine, strobe light,
scary music, etc.). On Halloween night, I've been known to don a
spooky costume and hide in bushes around the neighborhood in order to
jump out and scare trick-or-treaters.

As I introduce my Halloween letterbox series let me make this
perfectly clearI'm a believer in a scary Halloween. You won't find
any smiling ghosts or cutesy benevolent witches in my Halloween
Seriesit's scary! So if you letterbox with an impressionable,
possibly nightmare-prone child (or adult . . .), you may want to
reconsider tackling this series.

#1 Suicide Bridge
This beautiful 1,467 foot concrete bridge is the scenic gateway into
downtown Pasadena, just northeast of downtown Los Angeles. It is also
well-known to Angelenos as "Suicide Bridge."

Constructed in 1913, the 150 ft. high structure crosses the dry
Arroyo Seco riverbed. A decades-old story maintains that the body of
a worker who toppled into a just-poured concrete support could not be
recovered and remains entombed in the bridge to this day.
Supposedly, his soul cries out to other desparate beings. While this
particular story is unfounded, a number of men did die awful deaths
in the bridge's construction. This may explain why the bridge became
a hot spot of sorts during the early part of the last century and the
Depression when 95 people jumped to their deaths between 1919 and
1937.

One of the most famous suicides occurred on May 1, 1937, when a
despondent mother threw her baby girl over the railing and then
jumped over herself. The mother died, but miraculously, the little
girl survived the plunge. Her mother had inadvertently tossed her
into some nearby trees, and she was later recovered from the thick
branches.

Exit the 210 Freeway at Orange Grove Avenue in Pasadena and drive
south to the corner of Colorado Boulevard and Orange Grove. From
this point, travel one block south on Orange Grove to Green Street.
Turn right on Green Street for one block, then right on Grand for one
block until you come to a circular dead end in a park area. Park
your car where you are allowed (check the signs), and walk along the
paved path west toward the bridge. On the bridge, you will see
regularly-spaced, inset benches. Count them until you get to bench
number six on the bridge's south side; here, sit and rest, and notice
that you are at the one of the bridge's highest pointsthe place
where someone committing suicide would have likely climbed over the
railing. In front of you, separating the sidewalk from the street,
are double metal guardrails mounted on evenly-spaced metal posts.
Look beneath the lower rail behind the left-most post you see in
front of you for the letterbox.

From: Dawnette (rbngdfello@aol.com) | Date: 2003-10-27 03:47:00 UTC
We started this series today and had a blast. The series was
admittedly creepy, but we kept the creepiest details from the
smallest fry in our group, so Watermelon won't have nightmares.

Here is a (ahem) sit-rep on the Halloween series:

~Suicide Bridge--alive and well (pun intended) WARNING: despite
vigilance on our part and the fact that both girls watched me squat
down on the pedestrian side of the rail to retrieve the box, BOTH
girls managed to lean forward over the guard rail to look for where
it had been hidden, thereby putting their heads right out into
traffic. We had just chewed out the older one when the younger one
tried it. Thank heavens there were no cars going by either time! If
you take kids, be warned that this maneuver appears to be an
irresistible kid temptation, and traffic, when there is any, whizzes
by awfully close. Also, this is an isolated area with some old,
abandoned buildings at the east end of the bridge. There are homeless
folks in the area. I was glad G String was with me and I wasn't
alone with the kids in the middle of the bridge when a large homeless
man shuffled by. (BTW, we got some odd looks for having kids on the
bridge in the first place. Found the whole thing creepy--as
intended!)

~Rialto Theater--didn't find the box. Don't know whether it is
missing, I was looking in the wrong spot, or whether someone else had
it and was stamping in. I felt I was drawing a lot of attention to
the spot, so we bailed . . .

~Memorial Park Moon--alive and well. Had to dodge a pug rescue event
in the park, but managed this easily.

~Memorial Park Scaredy Cat--alive and well and guarded by a squirrel

~Charmed Witch--alive and well. Had to tiptoe through the KidSpace
Halloween festivities to get there because we walked over from
Memorial Park.

~Halloween Ghost--didn't budget our time well and arrived about 7
minutes too late to enter, so will have to visit Mr. Woodson next
weekend.

Ours were the first stamps in the four boxes we found.

This series was nicely done. Obviously a LOT of thought went into the
whole project. The stamps were lovely and varied and related nicely
both to the theme and the locations. (We particularly liked the Moon
stamp!) In all, 5 thumbs up!

It was a nice, low exercise way to get away from the worst of the
smoke and ash. Many thanks!

~Doughnut, G String, Moody Cat, Watermelon, and Jelly Bean

Doughnut,
I visited the Rialto Theater today, and am relieved to report that my
letterbox is still in place. When you walk down the concrete incline
behind the theater, walk up to the bottom step of the metal stairs in
front of you. You are right, this letterbox is a magnetized box that
is stuck underneath the bottom step, at the front, right edge. This
step is so low you can't see the box, and if you reached underneath
and felt for it, you might think it's just part of the structure of
the stair step; like I said, great hiding place (as if anyone but a
crazy letterboxer is going to reach their hand underneath spider-
central). So along with my Ghost Stamp in Sierra Madre, that makes
two new stamps for your clan to look for.
--Buzzard (G Sting is a guitar string--oooohh, of course; how could I
have missed that? I would think a great signature stamp of a guitar
could be made for that name, but I don't remember seeing one.)
