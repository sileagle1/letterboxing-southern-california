From: kelsung42 (kelsung42@yahoo.com) | Date: 2009-01-06 09:48:48 UTC
Welcome to a wonderful hobby (hobbies!)

> I have a question if no one minds answering. We have a few stamps
that were bought but we my girls want to make their own. So would it
be bad if they made one and changed their stamp to the homemade one
from the bought one?

That would be great. There are no hard rules, so relax. I used a
tiny toy stamp for my first dozen finds, then carved a signature stamp
from some Speedy Cut, which is very crumbly so it didn't last. My
third stamp I carved from an eraser and used it for a couple hundred
boxes and exchanges at three events. Then I finally carved my newest
and largest signature stamp last Spring from "the pink stuff" which
has become my favorite medium.

> I can't seem to find the rubber and the carving tools are a bit
expensive right now after Christmas. So the carving tools will have to
wait. I do however have an exacto knife. Does anyone know where I
can find the big blocks of rubber, erasers?

Cheap "pink pearl" erasers are a good starting point, and an exacto
will do for now. When the budget permits you can check out more
specialized supplies.

www.letterboxing.org has lots of information. Check out the FAQ, as
well as the page on carving tips. There is also a chatlist like this
one specifically for new boxers, and I'm sure Don would be able to pop
in with a link to it. Mark and Sue Pepe maintain that list, and they
are really wonderful people.

> Does anyone have any tips? Like my girls are having some difficulty
stamping the pads on the rocks. We have tried using the actual
letterbox I even brought a little carrying box but it still doesn't do
a very good job of getting a good full complete stamp.

My logbook is a hardcover sketchbook I got at an art supply store, and
I use it as a level place to stamp into logbooks when in the wild.
You could carry any medium-sized hardcover book with you to use as a
surface, I suppose. I love it when a box is planted near a nice bench....

Kelsung

Welcome to a wonderful family game/sport/obsession ;-) Here is my
three-and-a-half cents worth of suggestions:

Use anything you want for your personal stamp(s); feel free to change
it/them later, if you want to. No one stays the same through their
Life; why should our LB icons be static? I keep saying that I need to
re-carve our personal stamp. One of these days, I'll actually *do* it!

You should look for a crafts store that offers classes, to see if any
of them offer something for carving. We have a Michaels a half-mile
from us; they occasionally offer a class in rubber stamping. Also,
many communities have Arts Centers that offer classes. Any such will
have tools that you can use while you are in the classroom, and you
can gain a little experience, thus helping you make a good choice when
you buy your own. And you might even find some used tools on sale for
very little money.

Ultimately, aside from such things as courtesy to others (which
includes picking up any trash you drop while on the trail), and being
stealthy in your hunting, even in the wilderness (you never know if a
hiker is coming from the other direction, even in chaparral-covered
SoCal), there are very few truly hard-and-fast rules about how you
box. We tend to be a little "stricter" than some, but that's in
regards to maintenance and such; your personal expression should be
your own, not what others think it should be.

BTW: if you ever decide to look westward for some boxing action, there
are some good LBs in the Santa Monica Mountains, and as soon as I can
get my act together and get the house cleaned up from the Holidays,
there will be more of them. I love to bake and I love wrapping
presents; I hate to clean up afterwards :-p

From: Curtis & Paula H. (8hands4jesus@dslextreme.com) | Date: 2009-01-06 23:29:11 UTC
Welcome aboard, Heather! We used store-bought stamps for a year
before biting the bullet and carving our own. Here's what we used:
white rubber eraser (easier than pink, I'm told) and an exacto
knife. Draw your design in pencil, then go over it real dark with
your pencil, then flip it over onto your eraser and scribble all over
the back side of the paper, thus transfering the design onto your
eraser. When deciding what to carve away and what to leave intact,
remember it's the intact portions that have contact with ink and
paper, and thus that's what shows up when you "stamp". Have fun!

Blessings,

hansenclan
