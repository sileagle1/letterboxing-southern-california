Circle X Ranch

From: kelsung42 (kelsung42@yahoo.com) | Date: 2009-01-19 09:04:12 UTC
In addition to my full-time day job, I do live sound and recording. I
guess you could call me a free-lance engineer. Due to recently
picking up some new regular work, my days off won't be anymore for a
while.

So today was going to be my last day letterboxing for probably quite a
while. I wanted to make it worth it, so I decided to seek out my Holy
Grail: "Circle X Ranch" the oldest box in California.

I drove out there early and took my time hiking, taking in some great
views on a perfect day. I was only the 8th finder of the box since
Don and PeterK replanted it in 2004.

Then I continued along the trail, heading for Tri-Peaks. PeterK had
also planted a box there, so I was after both. After a serious hike
up, I was blessed with an absolutely amazing view. I looked where the
clues instructed, but no box. I scoured the area, checking ever
possible rock and plant, but to no avail. I finally gave up, but
didn't feel disappointed. After all, I'd had a great hike on a
beautiful day and got an awesome view on the world below.

As I relaxed in the afternoon sun, I noted a small cave about halfway
up the sheer rock face that comprised the last fifty or so feet to the
peak. I decided I was going to get daring and climb up to take a look
inside. It was a hole about three feet in diameter that went back
about five feet into the rock, and lo and behold, there was the
tattered remains of a broken letterbox.

Upon further inspection, it appeared I was doing four hobbies
simultaneously. There were three weathered logbooks: PeterK's, an
apparent geocache, and a summit log full of notes from people who
climbed all the way up here. Torn bags, a soup can, pencil and after
digging around, the stamp. And at the base below where the box was
supposed to be I'd already taken a photo of a benchmark.

Letterboxing, geocaching, benchmark hunting, and a summit log all in
one place. And the reason was obvious: a million dollar view. I
replaced baggies for all the logs, but since the container was
smashed, I left it all in a freezer bag up in the cave out of the
weather and away from wildlife. I told Peter he should change the
listing to an extreme letterbox.

Then I spent the evening with Zuhl, Maule and Buffalo Tony at a comedy
show and late dinner. All in all, pretty much a perfect day.

Kelsung

Great story! This box has been high on our List for quite a while, as
well. We live just a few miles, straight-line, from here, and have
been up to Sandstone Peak in the past, but not in about three years.
There are several geocaches, including two Earthcaches that the
National Park Service set up, in the general area of Sandstone and
Tri-Peaks.

Perhaps we'll have to make our foray up there sooner rather than
later. We can bring a replacement container along for the LB :-) How
big was the original, do you think? Does it need a new logbook? And
should we put it back where the clues would have it (assuming we find
the right spot), or should we leave it in the little "cave". Can't
tell exactly which geocache that might be, but we'll find out when/if
we find it, and we can certainly set things up so that any cachers who
come along (pretty rare in that area, and most of them will know the
difference) won't disturb it.

What do the owners want us to do, if anything? We are quite willing
to give a go at fixing things, but if they would rather we not, that's
cool, especially if they are planning to go up there themselves. The
main question from our PoV is exactly *when* we would go up there.
Could be within a couple of weeks, or it might be rather later,
depending upon the weather.

Have to look for the benchmark, while we are at it :-) It isn't in
the NGS database; the nearest listed one is almost a mile away from
Sandstone Peak.
