Gouge! Modern Woodcut

From: aaaltomare (aaaltomare@gmail.com) | Date: 2009-02-04 16:57:46 UTC
The Hammer Museum at UCLA has, right now, an exhibition called Gouge!
The Modern Woodcut which chronicles select artists from 1870s through
now who choose to work with the woodcut or lino-cut medium. I just
read about this yesterday and plan to go on Thursday when the museum
is free to everyone. I wish I had found out earlier because the show
closes on the 8th. Woodcuts have always been fascinating to me and
even more so now that I have been expanding beyond the rubber into
wood and linoleum. Actively letterboxing has made me a better artist
because I start to look at everything differently; how would that
carve, how would the colors reduce to a simple black and white, what
kind of shading techniques can i use. My carving skills aren't great,
by any means, but I'm greatly improving with every new stamp I carve.
Has anyone else branched out from carving stamps to any other form of
art? Anyone here do linoleum or woodcuts? What about whittling?
whittling is another hobby that I explore. It's really rewarding,
wood to knife, seeing the three dimensional object start to take shape
with every curl of wood that hits the ground.

I'll let everyone know how the hammer was and if it was as inspiring
as I hope it will be.

-BT-

Re: Gouge! Modern Woodcut

From: aaaltomare (aaaltomare@gmail.com) | Date: 2009-02-06 23:27:25 UTC
Gouge was really great. I was a little disappointed by the final
room, but practically everything else was very cool. There were a few
pieces that were over 12 feet wide and one that must have been 15 feet
tall. They included a few of the actual woodcuts themselves, from
older pieces. Surprisingly they were in all really good condition.
They had one anonymous cut from the early 1900s that was carved by a
Monk and it was the cleanest thing I had ever seen. The recessed cuts
were so flat that you had to look really hard to see where the gouge
marks were. the exposed lines were so perfect it was hard to believe
that this was done by a human. As my friend Palmer pointed out "They
were monks, what else ..."

There were some neat contemporary pieces there too. A los angeles
area artist took images from a CCTV and using only one field of the
image transferred it to a piece of wood about 6 feet wide and just
carved out the scan lines. From far away it looks like a strange
photograph but upclose it looks like a zebra. Seeing the way the
artists tackled the faces gave me some ideas on how to improve the
facial features of my own carvings.

After seeing some of these masterworks (there were cuts from Munch,
Mattise and Picasso to name a few), I have seen quality work, carved
into rubber, sitting in tupperware containers...
