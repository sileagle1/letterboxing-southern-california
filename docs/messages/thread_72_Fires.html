I am 99.9% certain that the two boxes we placed in the Lake Arrowhead/San
Bernardino mountains are gone. Monday I saw the tree one was hidden under

apparently didn't hear us yelling to save the box. The historical landmark
sign would survive, in our opinion, so go for the box under the tree :)

In an effort to make chicken soup out of chicken sh*t, though, we are
planning to go visit/re-visit the boxes in the fire affect areas and
document how they fared. Right now, we're looking at maps and trying to
hypothesize about the expected condition of each box based upon it's
location in the fire zones. By our calculations, several of the Arrowhead
boxes not planted by us should still be alright - and hopefully my saying
that doesn't cause the fires to flare back up and go after those boxes as
well.

As of this morning, people were starting to evacuate Hesperia and 300 homes were burned on the north side of Lake Arrowhead. This thing is just burning out of control. At the rate things are going, the San Bernadino mountains may nothing but dirt and rock before this is over.

The onshore winds may be bringing in moisture but they are having the same effect as the Santa Anas but they are blowing the fires in the direction they hadn't been going previously.

I have heard a lot of reporters giving their opinions about when and where the fires will stop but I think there is no way of knowing until they actually go out. There is just too much fuel out there because of the bark beetles killing trees and the fact that some of these areas haven't burned in over 100 years.

>

I think we So.Cal-ers should think about a phoenix series. We could
each contribute a carving--our own version of a phoenix--and then see
about planting them in some of the cities affected by the fires as
they recover.

Even more care than usual would have to be taken to plant the boxes
in areas where foot traffic wouldn't harm the environment (i.e.,
contribute to erosion) and after a sufficient period of time so as
not add traffic that would be burdensome to those rebuilding.

Many of the fire areas are heavily dependent on tourism (Julian, Lake
Arrowhead, and Big Bear come to mind). I know many businesses in Big
Bear were already suffering because the lake has been so low in the
summer the past few years and the skiing hasn't been much to write
home about, either. For them to lose the business of many year-round
residents as well as that of the tourists will kill many a small
business. A few of our tourist dollars up there wouldn't hurt.

Thoughts, anyone?

From: Gwen & Don Jackson (foxsecurity@earthlink.net) | Date: 2003-10-30 19:59:39 UTC-08:00
I think that this idea should be considered very carefully. I know after our fire we were in a state of shock for quite a while, and we didn't lose any structure only 80 trees. We really didn't appreciate looky loos and it took us a while to get over the loss. Unless you go through a forest fire you do not have any idea what trauma it causes, at least we had no clue until we experienced it. Placing letterboxes at a fire scene may not be received well even though your intentions are good and I'm not sure we could generate enough tourist dollars to make a difference.
Don

some sense of normalcy, they are going to need visitors to get their
economies back on track although it will obviously be a while before
they get to that point.
