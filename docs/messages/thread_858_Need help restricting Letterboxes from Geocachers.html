I haven't planted many, due to location, but have had two go missing due to Geocaching - sooooo, now I do over kill. 
I make a point of mentioning "This is NOT a Geocache" on my lid note, I write on the back of the stamp itself, "Not swag, do not remove", AND to finish it off, I put it on the back of the log book as well. 
This may sound excessive, but recently the BSA decided to make Geocaching a Merit Badge and most of the places I have planted, or wanted to, are now covered in Geocaches from all the local Troops - not to mention all the Geocaches that were already there! 
By the way - I am not a Scout hater, been in for several years now.
Dezert Ratty

From: Adam (clarinet_226@yahoo.com) | Date: 2010-08-25 06:24:29 UTC
Not only should you put a note on the label and the stamp, but you should look up the geocachers names in your book, and look on the geocaching site for those people. The first cacher to sign in after a letterboxer should be the one who took the stamp. Contact that cacher, and you might get the stamp back. It didn't work for me, but it might for you.

From: Kelsung (kelsung42@yahoo.com) | Date: 2010-08-26 02:19:24 UTC
All good suggestions, especially the idea of contacting the cacher who might have your stamp. I registered with geocaching.com a couple years ago to keep track of what gets near my boxes. GPS is good to within 40 feet, so I have moved a couple 50 feet away from a nearby cache. When this wasn't feasible, labels help, but are often unnoticed or ignored. After repeated attacks on a couple, I've been forced to simply retire them. But currently, I have 4 boxes that coexist with a nearby cache with no problems at all.

In summary: label 'em, move 'em or retire 'em!

Kelsung

Re: Need help restricting Letterboxes from Geocachers

From: Libby Groom (reddgroom@roadrunner.com) | Date: 2010-08-26 06:43:49 UTC-07:00
Kelsung stated "GPS is good to within 40 feet" but that's an average and it's sometimes better and sometimes worse. Just like letterbox clues, in order to get a good accurate location fix, the owner must put some time and care into creating the proper instructions (coordinates) for the finders. Sometimes the owner makes typos or their coordinates are just plain off (by 100+ feet) and then you have to consider how determined geocachers are to find the prize. Most geocachers don't have 40 foot accuracy, their scope of searching could be 100-200 feet out around their coordinates ground zero spot.

Long story short... if your box is in the same general area as a geocache, the geocachers will probably wander around and find your box. (and your boxers may even wander around and find the geocache)

So... I agree: Label it, move it and/or retire it.

Reddgroom
